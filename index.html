Bridge the Divide

History

Racial inequality is rooted in historical contexts like colonialism and slavery, which created deep-seated disparities that persist today. The notion of "race" emerged and evolved significantly alongside European colonization and the transatlantic slave trade. The idea that "white" people were inherently superior to "non-white" people became a widespread belief. The treatment of indigenous people when Europeans landed on American shores demonstrates this. Policies like Segregaion, Apartheid, the Jim Crow Laws, the Chinese Excluion Acts and Immigration Quotas support the idea that one race is lesser than the other.

Areas of Impact

  • Economy: Disparities in income, wealth (e.g., due to denying services to specific neighborhoods based on race), employment, and entrepreneurship.
  • Education: Unequal school funding, achievement gaps, and disproportionate discipline for minority students.
  • Healthcare: Barriers to quality care, leading to worse health outcomes and higher rates of chronic diseases in minority communities (exacerbated by environmental racism).
  • Criminal Justice: Racial profiling, harsher sentencing, and disproportionate incarceration rates for people of color.
  • Housing: Persistent residential segregation and unequal neighborhood resources.
  • Politics: Voter suppression and underrepresentation in government.
  • Social/Psychological: Increased stress, anxiety, and trauma from discrimination, and limited social mobility

What Can You Do?

  • Education and Self-Reflection: Learn about the history of racism and become aware of unconscious biases and actively work to stop them in daily interactions and decision-making.
  • Advocacy and Activism: Vote and support elected officials who champion racial equity, speak up and challenge discriminatory actions, and support racial justice organizations, like the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) or the Amercian Civil Liberties Union (ACLU), by donating or volunteering.
  • Build Diverse Relationships: Broaden your social networks to include people from different racial and ethnic backgrounds and approach interactions with an open mind.
  • Support Minority-Owned Businesses: Intentionally purchase from and invest in businesses owned by people of color to help circulate wealth within those communities.

To learn more, you can visit: un.org/en/fight-racism or do your own research.
